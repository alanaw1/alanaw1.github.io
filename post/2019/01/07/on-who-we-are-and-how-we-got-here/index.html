Review of “Who We Are and How We Got Here”

2019/01/07

One of my goals this year is to read more books and to think critically about them. I’ve started a spreadsheet tracking books I’ve read and books I’m reading, and I’m excited to share it here.

I just finished reading David Reich’s book on ancient DNA (aDNA) and human history,

Reich, David. Who We Are and How We Got Here: Ancient DNA and the new science of the human past. Oxford University Press, 2018.

and decided to write about my thoughts. First of all, Reich’s book is particularly interesting and relevant for my work, because I often think about how statistical models can be introduced or implemented to analyze genetic data and to interrogate or investigate novel biological hypotheses or questions. My undergraduate work with my mentors Noah, Marc and Julia occasionally involved applications and analyses of the very same models that are implemented in the pipeline of aDNA studies bearing implications for human history. For example, I worked on mathematical properties of the site frequency spectrum (SFS), a summary statistic used to infer demographic history, and performed simulations of the coalescent with infinite sites mutation to generate SFS from a variety of demographic scenarios. More recently, TC1, Marc and I also proposed an explanation for the male-specific population bottleneck detected in the Old World five thousand years ago, which Reich discusses at length toward the end of the book (Chapter 10).

My involvement in human evolutionary genetic research made my reading of Reich’s book more focused, so as I read my thoughts often oscillated between “big picture” arguments and scientific details (e.g., how many samples did they use in their four-population test? How can we differentiate fitness differences arising from inequality versus from competition when detecting sex-biased admixture?). Because I was frequently reflecting “on two levels,” I found the read particularly enjoyable.

I summarize my three main takeaways from the book below.

Takeaway 1: Readable big-picture summary of the aDNA literature

Prior to reading the book, I knew very little about how aDNA was used to infer population admixtures, apart from having vague impressions about counting the number of mutations differentiating one population sample from another (sloppily picked up from skimming through papers here and there). What Reich does—explaining the basic idea behind the three- and four-population tests and supplying loads of examples (Africa, East Asia, Eurasia, India) that are spread across individual chapters—helped drill the logic of the argument into my head very effectively. Additionally, he cites enough references for one to be able to further pursue any particular question in a scientific manner. As Reich himself puts it, the book is meant for “archaeologists, anthropologists, historians, linguists, and others eager to come to grips with the ancient DNA revolution.” Insofar as knowledge flourishes from new insights discovered by scholars on whose work aDNA studies impact, Reich is doing multiple scholarly communities a huge service.

Takeaway 2: Tricky foray into race and ancestry

Towards the end of the book (Chapter 11), Reich points out the “false dichotomy” between non-geneticists critical of the work of population geneticists (population geneticists “have invented a set of euphemisms for to discuss topics that had become taboo”) and population geneticists themselves who claim that “biological differences among populations are so modest that as a matter of social policy they can be ignored.” The taboo topic referred to here being racism/eugenics, Reich is worried that data analyses in the near future may reveal meaningful biological differences, which will shoot down the population geneticists’ claim and open a can of worms that previously politically correct scientists will have lost their credibility to dismiss from scientific higher ground.

This is tricky business, because it not only invites individuals who hold prior beliefs about biological superiority of certain races over others to comment in support of Reich’s point of worry, but also forces well-intentioned geneticists to point out to these individuals their racist prior beliefs. Reich wrote an op-ed about this, which bioengineering professor Ian Holmes considers a piece of poor communication.

Regardless, I think Reich has good intentions and I believe that the present scientific basis of biological superiority is in fact unrigorous pseudoscience. There are many issues with arguments about biological superiority that are supposedly backed by genetic studies. To be precise, I consider within these issues phenotypic traits that typically imply biological superiority, e.g., intelligence, athleticism., hardworking qualities. The issues are enumerated below.

  1. Association does not imply causation. Genetic contributions to phenotypic traits do not by themselves elucidate a mechanism by which the genes affect the phenotype.
  2. Apart from genetic contributions, environmental contributions can matter and can moreover interact with genetic contributions to influence phenotypic outcomes. The mechanism of interaction should ideally also be identified.

For any claims of biological superiority to be scientifically valid, therefore, it is necessary for the associated trait to be illustrably derived from a biological process involving the genes present, and moreover for the environment to have little to no role in this mechanistic process.

A recent commentary by Noah on the interpretation of polygenic scores and human phenotypic differences articulates the second point. Doc2, who co-authored the piece, went onto Twitter to explain and (later) to summarize his response to some pointed discussions on his earlier explanatory tweets. I highly recommend reading his tweets.

Takeaway 3: aDNA can detect ghost populations

One surprising use of aDNA is in detecting ghost populations, which are ancient populations whose data is unavailable, but whose past existence can be inferred based on genetic contributions to later people. Ghost populations have been detected multiple times using aDNA:

  • One population that branches away from the ancestral clade of Denisovans, Neanderthals and modern humans
  • Ancient North Eurasians that admixed into both northern Europeans and Native Americans about 25 thousand years ago (kya)
  • Yangtze River and Yellow River ghost populations that existed between 10 and 5 kya, who spreaded the Austroasiatic languages and the Sino-Tibetan languages respectively

These discoveries are insightful in themselves. However, what fascinated me was how geneticists managed to use ghost population information to place new archaeological findings within an evolutionary context. For instance, Eske Willerslev’s team discovered bones of a boy who had lived at Mal’ta in Siberia about 24 kya, and the genetic data matched expectations of the Ancient North Eurasian ghost population. Willerslev’s discovery effectively provided geographical evidence and information about the ghost population. Given the amount of population history already learned from aDNA studies, probably more archaeological discoveries can be placed in evolutionary contexts that help situate ghost populations geographically.
