Lagrange Multipliers Revisited

2019/01/04

Happy New Year! This is my first post, and I intend to use this space to write about my thoughts and learning. Any constructive feedback would be appreciated!

Now for the actual post. IŌĆÖve been thinking about mathematics over Winter break, mostly because I often find myself overlooking details about important results and consequently learning maths not as thoroughly as IŌĆÖd like. I find this especially true with regard to analysis (or continuous maths in general), so I try to revisit the maths every now and then. My most recent thoughts were centred on Lagrange multipliers, motivated by coursework in statistics.

Statistics and optimization share a close relationship, starting with the maximization of the maximum likelihood estimate in numerous settings, and featuring applications of Bayesian approaches to minimize regret functions in, e.g., universal prediction problems, amongst various areas of research. Hence, I wasnŌĆÖt surprised to learn recently, in the context of theoretical statistics, that exponential families maximize entropy:

Theorem 1 (Exponential families maximize entropy). For a random variable \(X\) with density \(p\), let \[H(p) = \mathbb{E}_p(-\log p(X))=-\int_{\{x:p(x)>0\}} p(x)\log(p(x))d\mu(x).\] Suppose that \(\mathbb{E}_p[T(X)]=\alpha\) for some \(T:\mathcal{X}\rightarrow \mathbb{R}^s\) and \(\alpha\in \text{int}(\text{conv}(T(\mathcal{X})))\). Let \(\eta\in \mathbb{R}^s\) and \(p_\eta(x) = \exp(\langle\eta,T(x)\rangle - A(\eta))\) (an \(s\)-parameter exponential family). Then \(H(p)\) is maximized over the set of all densities \(p\) satisfying \(\mathbb{E}_p[T(X)]=\alpha\), by setting \(p=p_\eta\) with \(\eta\) chosen to satisfy the constraints.

The proof of Theorem 1 above uses the method of Lagrange multipliers. In the case of \(\mathcal{X}\) finite, we have the Lagrangian \[L(p,\lambda,\eta_0,\eta_1,\ldots,\eta_s)=\sum_{i=1}^n p_i\log(1/p_i) + \sum_{j=1}^s\left(\alpha_j - \sum_{i=1}^n p_iT_j(x_i)\right) + \eta_0\left(\sum_{i=1}^n p_i - 1\right)-\sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_ip_i,\] which upon setting \(\nabla L=0\) (i.e., set the partial derivatives to zero), yields \[p_\text{opt} = \exp\left(-1 + \sum_{j=1}^s \eta_jT_j(x_i) +\eta_0-\lambda_i\right).\] ItŌĆÖs a straightforward exercise to verify the complementary slackness conditions and to obtain the exponential family as an extremal. The proof of the maximality of this extremal relies on JensenŌĆÖs inequality, which completes the argument. The case where \(\mathcal{X}\) is infinite (e.g., \(\mathcal{X}=\mathbb{R}\)) is more complicated, but at least we were able to verify a special case of Theorem 1 with only the method of Lagrange multipliers and elementary convexity theory at our disposal.

Why Lagrange Multipliers Work

In a sense, we were lucky because the problem involved a convex function. What happens if we minimize (which is, modulo a multiple of \(-1\), equivalent to maximizing) a function \(f(x)\) subject to the constraints \(F(x)=0\), where \(f\) is smooth but not necessarily convex? In a realistic setting, it is sometimes not possible to inspect the function and to know immediately that it is convex or concave. When this happens, more work is required in the application of Lagrange multipliers to justify the minimality. I decided to revisit this problem, and the theory of Lagrange multipliers more generally, by reading some textbooks. I found some good answers, but none so far has exceeded the clarity of Vladimir ZorichŌĆÖs presentation1. In what follows, I summarize his explanation, which I found helpful for my review of the theory of Lagrange multipliers.

We consider a more general setting, which is to optimize a function \(f:D\rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) defined on an open set \(D\subset \mathbb{R}^n\) and belonging to \(C^p(D;\mathbb{R})\) (\(p\geqslant 1\)), subject to \(x\in S\) for some smooth surface \(S\subset D\).

  • Example 1: \(f(x,y,z)=x^2+yz\in C^1(\mathbb{R}^3;\mathbb{R})\) and \(S=\{(x,y,z):x+y+z=1\}\), i.e., we optimize \(f\) on the plane \(S\).
  • Example 2: More generally, \(D=\mathbb{R}^n\) and the surface \(S\) is parametrized by the system \[\begin{eqnarray} F^1(x_1,\ldots,x_n) & = & 0, \\ \nonumber \vdots & \vdots & \vdots \\ \label{system} F^m(x_1,\ldots,x_n) & = & 0,\nonumber \end{eqnarray}\] and we optimize \(y=f(x_1,\ldots,x_n)\) over \(S\).

The following lemma justifies the formulation of the Lagrangian.

Lemma 2 (Zorich, p.┬Ā528) A necessary condition for a point \(x_0\in S\) that is noncritical for \(f\) to be a local extremum of \(f|_S\) is that \(T_{x_0}S\subset T_{x_0}N\), where \(T_{x_0}S\) is the tangent space to the surface \(S\) at \(x_0\) and \(T_{x_0}N\) is the tangent space to the level surface \(N=\{x\in D: f(x)=f(x_0)\}\) of \(f\) to which \(x_0\) belongs.

The noncriticality of \(f(x_0)\) is required for the proof to follow through. However, in downstream applications of the Lagrange multipliers method, this requirement ends up being non-essential. Indeed, if the point \(x_0\in D\) were a critical point of the function \(f\) or an extremum of the function, it is a potential candidate for extrema of \(f|_S\), the function of interest. The point of noncriticality is that the function \(f|_S\) may have extrema that are different from those of \(f\), and we wish to find these points methodically.

To prove Lemma 2, we start by choosing any vector \(\xi\in T_{x_0}S\). By definition of tangent space, we can find a smooth path \(x=x(t)\) on \(S\) passing through \(x_0\) at \(t=0\) and for which the vector \(\xi\) is the velocity at \(t=0\): \(dx/dt|_{t=0}=\xi\). Since \(f\) is smooth (at least differentiable) and \(x_0\) is an extremum of \(f|_S\), the smooth function \(f(x(t))\) has an extremum at \(t=0\), and the chain rule implies \[\nabla f(x_0)\cdot \xi=0,\] where \(\nabla f(x_0)=\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1},\ldots,\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n}\right)\). This last equality is just the equation of the tangent space \(T_{x_0}N\), as long as \(f(x_0)\) is not critical, and so \(\xi\in T_{x_0}N\).

Notice that when Lemma 2 is applied to Example 2, recall that the space \(T_{x_0}S\) of the noncritical point \(x_0\) of \(f\) is defined by2 the system of linear equations \[\begin{eqnarray} \frac{\partial F^1}{\partial x_1}(x_0)\xi_1 + \ldots + \frac{\partial F^1}{\partial x_n}(x_0)\xi_n & = & 0, \\ \nonumber \vdots & \vdots & \vdots\\ \label{tangentspace} \frac{\partial F^m}{\partial x_1}(x_0)\xi_1 + \ldots + \frac{\partial F^m}{\partial x_n}(x_0)\xi_n & = & 0, \\ \nonumber \end{eqnarray}\]

so that the set of vectors \(\mathcal{S}=\{\nabla F^k(x_0)\}_{k=1}^m\) satisfies \[\mathcal{S}^\perp\subset \{\nabla f(x_0)\}^\perp.\] Taking perp on both sides, we see that \(\text{span}(\nabla f(x_0))\subset \text{span}(\mathcal{S})\), which in particular implies that there exist linear coefficients \(\lambda_k\in \mathbb{R}\) such that \[\nabla f(x_0) = \sum_{k=1}^m\lambda_k \nabla F^k(x_0).\] Letting \[L(x,\lambda)=f(x) - \sum_{k=1}^m\lambda_k F^k(x),\] we see that \(\nabla L(x,\lambda)|_{x=x_0}=0\). Thus, we showed the following:

For a noncritical point \(x_0\) of \(f\) to be a local extremum of \(f|_S\) (with \(S\) defined by the system of linear equations in Example 2), it is necessary for real constants \(\lambda_k\) to exist such that the gradient \(\nabla L(x,\lambda)\) is zero at \(x_0\).

This \(L\) function is called the Lagrangian, and the discussion above justifies why we formulate the Lagrangian and set its gradient to zero in computational settings.

From Necessity to Sufficiency

We have seen a necessary condition for a noncritical point \(x_0\) of the objective function \(f\) to be an extremum on \(S\). This only allows us to obtain candidates for the extremum of \(f|_S\), without knowing if the candidate is a true extremum, and (if it were) whether the extremum is a minimum or a maximum. There is a sufficient condition for the presence or absence of a true constrained minimum or maximum, and we state it below. It requires the objective function \(f\) and the surface \(S\) to be sufficiently smooth.

Theorem 3 (Zorich, p.┬Ā533) Let \(f:D\rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) be in \(C^2(D;\mathbb{R})\), \(S\subset D\) be the surface defined by the relations \(\{F^k(x)=0\}_{k=1}^m\), where \(F^k\in C^2(D;\mathbb{R})\). Suppose that for a extremum candidate point \(x_0\) of \(f|_S\), the parameters \(\lambda_1,\ldots,\lambda_m\) in the Lagrangian \(L(x,\lambda)\), solved by setting \(\nabla L=0\), are unique.3 Then, a sufficient condition for the point \(x_0\) to be a true extremum is that the Hessian \[\text{Hess}_x(L) = \left(\frac{\partial^2 L}{\partial x_i\partial x_j}(x_0)\right)_{1\leqslant i,j\leqslant n}\] is positive-definite or negative-definite on \(T_{x_0}S\), i.e., \(\sum_{i,j}\frac{\partial^2 L}{\partial x_i\partial x_j}\frac{\partial^2 L}{\partial x_i\partial x_j}(x_0)\xi_i\xi_j\) does not change sign over the range \(\xi\in T_{x_0}S\). If \(\text{Hess}_x(L)\) is positive-definite on \(T_{x_0}S\), then \(x_0\) is a strict local minimum of \(f|_S\); if it is negative-definite, then \(x_0\) is a strict local maximum. Otherwise, \(x_0\) is not a true extremum.

Here are examples applying Lemma 2 and Theorem 3. (They are taken from pp.┬Ā536-537 of Zorich.)

  • Example 3: Suppose \(f(x,y,z)=x^2-y^2+z^2\) and we wish to extremize \(f\) on \(S\), defined by \(F(x,y,z)=2x-y-3=0\). The Lagrangian is \[L(x,y,z,\lambda)=(x^2-y^2+z^2) - \lambda(2x-y-3),\] and the necessary conditions for an extremum, obtained by setting partial derivatives of \(L\) to zero, leads to a candidate point \(p=(2,1,0)\). We apply Theorem 3. The tangent space of \(S\) is the set of vectors \(\xi=(\xi_1,\xi_2,\xi_3)\) satisfying \((\partial_x F, \partial_y F,\partial_z F)|_{(2,1,0)}^\top\xi=2\xi_1 - \xi_2 = 0\), and the quadratic form computed from \(\xi^\top \text{Hess}_{(x,y,z)}(L)\xi\), is \(2\xi_1^2 -2\xi_2^2 + 2\xi_3^2= -6\xi_1^2 + 2\xi_3^2\). Since \(\xi_1\) and \(\xi_3\) are independent variables, we see that the quadratic form changes sign over \(T_{(2,1,0)}S\), and so the candidate point isnŌĆÖt a true extremum. (Indeed, it can be verified easily that \(f|_S\) ranges over \(\mathbb{R}\).)
  • Example 4: Suppose \(f(x,y)=x^2-y^2\) and we wish to extremize \(f\) on \(S\), defined by \(F(x,y,z)=2x-y-3=0\). Setting \(\nabla L=0\) yields \(p=(2,1)\) as a candidate point. The tangent space \(T_{x_0}S\) is (after dropping the third coordinate \(\xi_3\)) the same as in Example 3, but the quadratic form is now \(\xi^\top \text{Hess}_{(x,y)}(L)\xi=2\xi_1^2-2\xi_2^2 = -6\xi_1^2\), which is non-negative no matter how \(\xi\) is chosen. Applying Theorem 3, we conclude that \(\text{Hess}_{(x,y)}(L)\) is negative-definite and \(p=(2,1)\) is a local maximum of \(f|_S\).

From Local Extremum to Global Extremum

Theorem 3 above enables the extremum property of candidates of the objective function \(f\) to be verified. Suppose we found a candidate point \(p\) of \(f\) by differentiating the Lagrangian, and verified its extremality using Theorem 3. We still do not know if the extremum is merely a local extremum or is in fact the global extremum.

There are some general methods to verify whether an extremum found is indeed global.

  1. If the objective function \(f\) is convex (resp., concave) and differentiable over a domain \(D\), then any critical point \(x_0\) in the interior of \(D\) is a global minimum (resp., maximum). This is exemplified by the exponential family example we considered in the beginning of this post, and is powerful in general because it allows one to use ŌĆ£visual inspectionŌĆØ to conclude global extremality when the convexity or concavity of \(f\) is apparent.
  2. If we donŌĆÖt know \(f\) is convex or concave, then we have to rely on the surface \(S\subset D\) over which \(f\) is optimized. If \(f\) is continuous (which we assume in most cases, and is moreover required for Lemma 2 and Theorem 3 to work), then its global extrema is attained at the boundary of \(S\) or in the interior. In case a global extrema is attained in the interior, it must be a critical point (otherwise, we can perturb \(x\) slightly to increase or decrease its value and contradict extremality).

So, the second method is useful when we do not know anything about the convexity of \(f\). Moreover, it is also more general, in the sense that, even with a convex (resp., concave) and continuous \(f\), we can still apply the second method to verify minimality (resp., maximality). Thus, the second method is more useful to remember.

  • Example 5: (Taken from Gelca and AndreescuŌĆÖs Putnam and Beyond) Find the smallest constant \(k>0\) such that \[\frac{xy}{x+y+2z} +\frac{yz}{y+z+2x} + \frac{zx}{z+x+2y}\leqslant k(x+y+z),\] for \(x,y,z>0\). We will show that \(k_{\min}=1/4\). First, the inequality remains unchanged on replacing \(x,y,z\) by \(tx,ty,tz\) with \(t>0\), so we may assume that \(x+y+z=1\) and \(k=\sup f\), where \[f(x,y,z)=\frac{xy}{x+y+2z} +\frac{yz}{y+z+2x} + \frac{zx}{z+x+2y}.\] and \((x,y,z)\in \Delta_2 = \{(a,b,c):a+b+c=1,a,b,c>0\}\). The Lagrangian \(L(x,y,z,\lambda)\) is \(f(x,y,z) - \lambda(x+y+z-1)\), and solving the system of equations of \(\nabla L = 0\) yields only one candidate \(p=(1/3,1/3,1/3)\). (See p.┬Ā173 of Gelca and Andreescu for computational details.) The tangent space, obtained by the standard method discussed in the previous section, is \(T_{(1/3,1/3,1/3)}\Delta_2=\{\xi:\xi_1+\xi_2+\xi_3=0\}\), and it can be checked that the quadratic form \(\xi^\top\text{Hess}_{(x,y,z)}(L)\xi=-3(\xi_1^2+\xi_2^2+\xi_3^2)\leqslant 0\). By Theorem 3, \(p\) is a local maximum. Moreover, \(f(p) = 1/4\). Because we cannot easily tell if \(f\) is convex, we apply the second method to verify that \(p\) is a global maximum. The only other candidates for global maximum are other candidate extrema identified by differentiating the Lagrangian (none) or the boundary points of \(\Delta_2\). The boundary points of \(\Delta_2\) are points of the form \((x,y,0),(0,y,z),(x,0,z)\), with the two free variables ranging over \(\Delta_1=\{(a,b):a+b=1,a,b,>0\}\). (These points arenŌĆÖt contained in \(\Delta_2\)!) It is straightforward to check that when this happens, \(f\) attains its maximum when the two free variables are equal to \(1/2\) and each other. Moreover, at these points, \(f=1/4\). Thus, we see that \(f\) is bounded above by \(1/4\) and moreover attains \(1/4\) at \((1/3,1/3,1/3)\), which completes the argument.

What Else?

IŌĆÖve revisited the method of Lagrangian multipliers, outlining a rigorous mathematical explanation behind why it makes sense and (hopefully) providing concrete and instructive examples that show the abstract theorems and lemma in action.

All of the theory discussed so far only applies to optimization over surface constraints. More generally, we may encounter problems involving optimization over constraint sets involving inequalities (i.e., \(F(x)\leqslant 0\)), such as discs, tori and convex bodies. The theory that deals with this is an extension of the Lagrange multipliers method, and involves concepts like complementary slackness and the Karush-Kuhn-Tucker conditions.

Adding on, we only discussed (see Footnote 3) the LICQ as a sufficient condition for Theorem 3 to work. The optimization theory literature is filled with other conditions that guarantee extremality of candidate points.

IŌĆÖll try to write about these two extensions sometime in the future. For now, thanks for reading and staying with me so far!
