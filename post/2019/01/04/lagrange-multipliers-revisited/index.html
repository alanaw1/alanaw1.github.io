Lagrange Multipliers Revisited

2019/01/04

Happy New Year! This is my first post, and I intend to use this space to write about my thoughts and learning. Any constructive feedback would be appreciated!

Now for the actual post. I’ve been thinking about mathematics over Winter break, mostly because I often find myself overlooking details about important results and consequently learning maths not as thoroughly as I’d like. I find this especially true with regard to analysis (or continuous maths in general), so I try to revisit the maths every now and then. My most recent thoughts were centred on Lagrange multipliers, motivated by coursework in statistics.

Statistics and optimization share a close relationship, starting with the maximization of the maximum likelihood estimate in numerous settings, and featuring applications of Bayesian approaches to minimize regret functions in, e.g., universal prediction problems, amongst various areas of research. Hence, I wasn’t surprised to learn recently, in the context of theoretical statistics, about how exponential families maximize entropy:

Theorem 1 (Exponential families maximize entropy). For a random variable \(X\) with density \(p\), let \[H(p) = \mathbb{E}_p(-\log p(X))=-\int_{\{x:p(x)>0\}} p(x)\log(p(x))d\mu(x).\] Suppose that \(\mathbb{E}_p[T(X)]=\alpha\) for some \(T:\mathcal{X}\rightarrow \mathbb{R}^s\) and \(\alpha\in \text{int}(\text{conv}(T(\mathcal{X})))\). Let \(\eta\in \mathbb{R}^s\) and \(p_\eta(x) = \exp(\langle\eta,T(x)\rangle - A(\eta))\) (an \(s\)-parameter exponential family). Then \(H(p)\) is maximized over the set of all densities \(p\) satisfying \(\mathbb{E}_p[T(X)]=\alpha\), by setting \(p=p_\eta\) with \(\eta\) chosen to satisfy the constraints.

The proof of Theorem 1 above uses the method of Lagrange multipliers. In the case of \(\mathcal{X}\) finite, we have the Lagrangian \[L(p,\lambda,\eta_0,\eta_1,\ldots,\eta_s)=\sum_{i=1}^n p_i\log(1/p_i) + \sum_{j=1}^s\left(\alpha_j - \sum_{i=1}^n p_iT_j(x_i)\right) + \eta_0\left(\sum_{i=1}^n p_i - 1\right)-\sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_ip_i,\] which upon setting \(\nabla L=0\) (i.e., set the partial derivatives to zero), yields \[p_\text{opt} = \exp\left(-1 + \sum_{j=1}^s \eta_jT_j(x_i) +\eta_0-\lambda_i\right).\] It’s a straightforward exercise to verify the complementary slackness conditions and to obtain the exponential family as an extremal. The proof of the maximality of this extremal relies on Jensen’s inequality, which completes the argument. The case where \(\mathcal{X}\) is infinite (e.g., \(\mathcal{X}=\mathbb{R}\)) is more complicated, but at least we were able to verify a special case of Theorem 1 with only the method of Lagrange multipliers and elementary convexity theory at our disposal.

What happens without convexity?

In a sense, we were lucky because the problem involved a convex function. What happens if we minimize (which is, modulo a multiple of \(-1\), equivalent to maximizing) a function \(f(x)\) subject to the constraints \(F(x)=0\), where \(f\) is smooth but not necessarily convex? In a realistic setting, it is sometimes not possible to inspect the function and to know immediately that it is convex or concave. I read some analysis textbooks and found answers, but none so far has exceeded the clarity of Vladimir Zorich’s presentation1. In what follows, I summarize his explanation, which I found helpful for my review of the theory of Lagrange multipliers.

We consider a more general setting, which is to optimize a function \(f:D\rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) defined on an open set \(D\subset \mathbb{R}^n\) and belonging to \(C^p(D;\mathbb{R})\) (\(p\geqslant 1\)), subject to \(x\in S\) for some smooth surface \(S\subset D\).

  • Example 1: \(f(x,y,z)=x^2+yz\in C^1(\mathbb{R}^3;\mathbb{R})\) and \(S=\{(x,y,z):x+y+z=1\}\), i.e., we optimize \(f\) on the plane \(S\).
  • Example 2: More generally, \(D=\mathbb{R}^n\) and the surface \(S\) is parametrized by the system \[\begin{eqnarray} F^1(x_1,\ldots,x_n) & = & 0, \\ \nonumber \vdots & \vdots & \vdots \\ \label{system} F^m(x_1,\ldots,x_n) & = & 0,\nonumber \end{eqnarray}\] and we optimize \(y=f(x_1,\ldots,x_n)\) over \(S\).

The following lemma justifies the formulation of the Lagrangian.

Lemma 2 (Zorich, p. 528) A necessary condition for a point \(x_0\in S\) that is noncritical for \(f\) to be a local extremum of \(f|_S\) is that \(T_{x_0}S\subset T_{x_0}N\), where \(T_{x_0}S\) is the tangent space to the surface \(S\) at \(x_0\) and \(T_{x_0}N\) is the tangent space to the level surface \(N=\{x\in D: f(x)=f(x_0)\}\) of \(f\) to which \(x_0\) belongs.

The noncriticality of \(f(x_0)\) is required for the proof to follow through. However, in downstream applications of the Lagrange multipliers method, this requirement ends up being non-essential. Indeed, if the point \(x_0\in D\) were a critical point of the function \(f\) or an extremum of the function, it is a potential candidate for extrema of \(f|_S\), the function of interest. The point of noncriticality is that the function \(f|_S\) may have extrema that are different from those of \(f\), and we wish to find these points methodically.

To prove Lemma 2, we start by choosing any vector \(\xi\in T_{x_0}S\). By definition of tangent space, we can find a smooth path \(x=x(t)\) on \(S\) passing through \(x_0\) at \(t=0\) and for which the vector \(\xi\) is the velocity at \(t=0\): \(dx/dt|_{t=0}=\xi\). Since \(f\) is smooth (at least differentiable) and \(x_0\) is an extremum of \(f|_S\), the smooth function \(f(x(t))\) has an extremum at \(t=0\), and the chain rule implies \[\nabla f(x_0)\cdot \xi=0,\] where \(\nabla f(x_0)=\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1},\ldots,\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n}\right)\). This last equality is just the equation of the tangent space \(T_{x_0}N\), as long as \(f(x_0)\) is not critical, and so \(\xi\in T_{x_0}N\).

Notice that when Lemma 2 is applied to Example 2, recall that the space \(T_{x_0}S\) of the noncritical point \(x_0\) of \(f\) is defined by2 the system of linear equations \[\begin{eqnarray} \frac{\partial F^1}{\partial x_1}(x_0)\xi_1 + \ldots + \frac{\partial F^1}{\partial x_n}(x_0)\xi_n & = & 0, \\ \nonumber \vdots & \vdots & \vdots\\ \label{tangentspace} \frac{\partial F^m}{\partial x_1}(x_0)\xi_1 + \ldots + \frac{\partial F^m}{\partial x_n}(x_0)\xi_n & = & 0, \\ \nonumber \end{eqnarray}\]

so that the set of vectors \(\mathcal{S}=\{\nabla F^k(x_0)\}_{k=1}^m\) satisfies \[\mathcal{S}^\perp\subset \{\nabla f(x_0)\}^\perp.\] Taking perp on both sides, we see that \(\text{span}(\nabla f(x_0))\subset \text{span}(\mathcal{S})\), which in particular implies that there exist linear coefficients \(\lambda_k\in \mathbb{R}\) such that \[\nabla f(x_0) = \sum_{k=1}^m\lambda_k \nabla F^k(x_0).\] Letting \[L(x,\lambda)=f(x) - \sum_{k=1}^m\lambda_k F^k(x),\] we see that \(\nabla L(x,\lambda)|_{x=x_0}=0\). Thus, we showed the following:

For a noncritical point \(x_0\) of \(f\) to be a local extremum of \(f|_S\) (with \(S\) defined by the system of linear equations in Example 2), it is necessary for real constants \(\lambda_k\) to exist such that the gradient \(\nabla L(x,\lambda)\) is zero at \(x_0\).

This \(L\) function is called the Lagrangian, and the discussion above justifies why we formulate the Lagrangian and set its gradient to zero in computational settings.

The equation of the plane tangent to the graph of this function is, by Taylor’s theorem, \(z-z_0=\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(x_0,y_0)(x-x_0) + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y_0}(x_0,y_0)(y-y_0)\).
