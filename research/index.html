Research

Human Evolution

What do we know about our past? The study of human history is, and continues to be, transformed by the availability of genome data. The collection of new genomes along with their high-depth sequencing allow statistical models of evolution to be implemented on larger scales to reveal insights into how human populations migrated, admixed, or experienced population decline. Uncovering these evolutionary phenomena of our past enables us to learn more precisely about how we came to be, and the conclusions can be corrobarated with or challenge incumbent theories about human societies. For example, studies on genetic differentiation between worldwide populations refuted the multiple origins hypothesis and gave credence to the theory that all human populations descended from ancestors who lived in contintental Africa. I have worked on investigating a \(5000\)-year-old male-specific population decline inferred across populations in the Old World, contributing mathematical and computational models and synthesizing them with a diverse body of anthropological, sociological and archaeological studies to propose a reasonable explanation.

Population-Genetic Statistics

In the analysis of genetic data, summary statistics are often computed for biological interpretation. For example, single-nucleotide polymorphism (SNP) data is summarized into a sample frequency spectrum (SFS) for the inference of evolutionary or population history. Allele frequency statistics (like homozygosity \(J\) and population differentiation \(F_{ST}\)) reflect levels of genetic homogeneity and admixture. Pairs of summary statistics sometimes share a close mathematical relationship, so that the value of one statistic constrains the value of the other. When summary statistics are integrated with evolutionary models in genetic studies, knowing these constraints helps enhance biological interpretation by providing insight into how sensitive the summary statistics are to parameters of the model being considered. I have investigated the dependence of the SFS on various sample features including the number of segregating sites, the proportion of singleton alleles, and the sample size. I have also shown that the theory of majorization is a useful mathematical technique for quantifying constraints between pairs of statistics measuring genetic homogeneity and diversity.

Published Work

Combinatorics and Coding

In coding theory, finite metric spaces arise from defining distances between codewords. Because the transmission of codewords across a noisy channel causes errors to accumulate, distances between codewords are used to formulate rules for decoding noisy outputs of transmitted codewords. One such rule is nearest-neighbor (NN) decoding, which matches noisy outputs to the transmitted codeword that is nearest to it. Let \(C\) be a code, a subset of the metric space \(X\) of all possible messages. Assuming transmissions are restricted to elements of \(C\), there exist combinatorial quantities of \(C\) that provide information about the fidelity of NN decoding, such as the covering radius, packing radius and remoteness. (For example, the covering radius measures the largest number of noise-induced errors for which NN decoding does not produce an incorrect result.) I have obtained bounds on the covering radius for codes defined on sets of \(1\)-factors of complete uniform hypergraphs, as well as bounds on the \(m\)-covering radius — a generalization of the former — for codes defined on sets of permutations and sets of \(1\)-factors of complete uniform hypergraphs.

Mathematical and Computational Phylogenetics

In phylogenetics, which studies the evolutionary relationships among biological entities (species, individuals or genes), stochastic models are the generative models based on which statistical analyses of biological data (species counts, molecular-genetic data, etc.) are performed. I have worked on multiple questions in this area, including enumeration of coalescent histories (which describe viable paths for the merging of gene lineages in a coalescent model on a species tree) and bounding the number of loci required for species tree splits to appear in a set of gene trees (which guarantees statistical consistency in phylogenetic inference algorithms like ASTRAL I and II). For the former, I have obtained enumerative results via an order-theoretic approach; for the latter I have lower bounds on the number of loci required.
