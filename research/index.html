Human Evolution

Population-Genetic Statistics

In the analysis of genetic data, summary statistics are often computed for biological interpretation. For example, single-nucleotide polymorphism (SNP) data is summarized into a sample frequency spectrum (SFS) for the inference of evolutionary or population history. Allele frequency statistics (like homozygosity \(J\) and population differentiation \(F_{ST}\)) reflect levels of genetic homogeneity and admixture. Pairs of summary statistics sometimes share a close mathematical relationship, so that the value of one statistic constrains the value of the other. When summary statistics are integrated with population-genetic models, knowing these constraints helps enhance biological interpretation by providing insight into how sensitive the summary statistics are to parameters of the model being considered. I have investigated the dependence of the SFS on various sample features including the number of segregating sites, the proportion of singleton alleles, and the sample size. I have also shown that the theory of majorization is a useful mathematical technique for quantifying constraints between pairs of statistics measuring genetic homogeneity and diversity.
